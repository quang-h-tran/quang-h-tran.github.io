ABOUT

Hi, I'm Quang H. Tran

I am a 4th-year Ph.D. student in the Department of Astronomy at UT Austin, working with Prof. Brendan P. Bowler. I am a member of the inaugural cohort for the Exoplanet Explorers (ExoExplorers) Science Series, sponsored by the ExoPAG Executive Committee and the NASAâ€™s Exoplanet Exploration Program. Currently, I am at the Flatiron Institute's Center for Computational Astrophysics as a Pre-Doctoral Fellow working with Dr. Megan Bedell and Dr. Dan Foreman-Mackey.

I am primarily interested in the formation, migration, and evolution of planetary systems. I hunt for exoplanets using both ground- and space- based instruments such as the Habitable-Zone Planet (HPF) spectrograph and the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS).

My projects right now focus on searching for giant planets around young stars using near-infrared precision radial velocities. I am also working on characterizing, modelling, and mitigating stellar activity signatures found in astronomical data using Gaussian process regression. In general, I want to work on understanding underlying, population-level demographics of planets using probabilistic data analysis techniques.

Determining the Evolution and Migration of Young Giant Planets

I am carrying out a survey targeting intermediate age (~20-200 Myr) Sun-like (GK) stars with the near-infrared (NIR) Habitable-Zone Planet (HPF) spectrograph on the 10-meter Hobby-Eberly Telescope to determine when and how giant planets migrate to small separations.

It is currently not clear how giant planets interior to their host star's ice-line (~2.5 AU for a Sun-like star) arrived at their current location. Two migration processes can best explain this population of close-in giant planets: inspiraling disk migration or three-body dynamical interactions with an outer planet or stellar companion. These pathways can be distinguished by measuring the frequency of gas giants over time because they operate on different timescales. Thus, mapping when giant planets migrate is the key to determining how they migrate.

Young stars are intrinsically more magnetically active than older stars and induce non-dynamical astrophysical noise, which can mask radial velocity signals from planets and are difficult to identify and remove. Moving to NIR wavelengths has been shown to reduce these effects by a factor of ~2-3x for young T Tauri stars (~5 Myr). Focusing on intermediate age (~20-200 Myr) stars can further reduce this activity, opening the possibility of detecting close-in, giant, young planets.

This survey was selected for the 2019 Astrophysics Future Investigators in NASA Earth Space Science and Technology (FINESST) grant (80NSSC20K1554). The first paper describing the survey design, data reduction pipeline, and initial results is out and can be found here: The Epoch of Giant Planet Migration Planet Search Program I.
